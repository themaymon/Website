Statistical Power & The Tides

Statistics
Teaching
Methods
Why p-values are like spotting a rock in the ocean, and why we often miss the signal.
Author

Christopher Maymon

Published

May 21, 2025

One of the hardest concepts for psychology students to grasp is Statistical Power.

We teach them definitions like “1 - beta” or “the probability of rejecting the null hypothesis when it is false.” These definitions are accurate, but they are intuitive dead ends. They don’t help a student visualize why their experiment failed.

Instead, I prefer to teach Power using the metaphor of The Tides.

The Rock and The Water

Imagine you are standing on a cliff looking down at a turbulent ocean. Your goal is simple: Spot the Rock.

In this metaphor: * The Rock is your Effect Size. It is the real phenomenon you are trying to find. * The Water is the Noise (measurement error, individual differences). * The Tide Level is your Statistical Power.

Scenario A: Low Power (High Tide)

Imagine the tide is incredibly high. The water is deep and choppy. Somewhere beneath those waves, there is a rock. It is definitely there (the effect exists!), but the noise of the water is completely covering it.

You look for an hour and see nothing. You conclude: “There is no rock here.” In statistics, we call this a Type II Error (False Negative). The rock didn’t vanish; you just lacked the power to see it through the noise.

Scenario B: High Power (Low Tide)

Now, imagine we lower the tide. The water level drops. The waves settle. Suddenly, the jagged tip of the rock breaks the surface. You didn’t make the rock bigger; you just removed the noise that was hiding it.

This is what happens when we increase sample size or reduce measurement error (e.g., using a precise VR tracker instead of a stopwatch). We lower the water level so the signal becomes visible.

The “Big Rock” Fallacy

Many researchers try to fix a “High Tide” problem by looking for a “Bigger Rock.”

They think: “If I just make my manipulation stronger (a louder noise, a scarier VR cliff), the effect will be massive, and I’ll see it even if my sample is small.”

This works… sometimes. If the rock is a mountain, you will see it even at high tide. But most effects in psychology aren’t mountains; they are pebbles. We are looking for subtle changes in reaction time or slight shifts in emotion regulation.

If you are looking for a pebble at high tide, no amount of squinting (p-hacking) will save you. You need to drain the water.

Why Simulation Matters

This is why I advocate for Simulation Science.

In a traditional field study, the “weather” is unpredictable. The waves are choppy (high noise). In a Virtual Reality lab, I control the weather. I can turn off the wind. I can perfectly standardize the environment.

By using VR, we are essentially building a breakwall around our study. We calm the waters, reducing the noise variance, which makes even small rocks visible.

We don’t need “bigger” effects; we need calmer water.
